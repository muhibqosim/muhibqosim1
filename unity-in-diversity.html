An Overview of the Islamic Concept of Unity in Diversity and Diversity in Unity

Published on: 29th August, 2023

Introduction

It is trite principle of Islamic law that any act or regulation that negates the substantive law, notwithstanding wherever it emanates from, is null and void. The Islamic law, also the “Islamic Sharii`ah”, is a version of divine law binding on all adherents of the Islamic monotheism faith. It covers both civil and criminal matters. It is a code which regulates both the internal and external affairs of those subjected to its provisions. It is comprehensive so much so that nothing is left untouched and unregulated. It covers all things – the past, the present and the future. Its doctrines are sacrosanct, not open to amendment, free from inadequacy, weaknesses and loopholes.

In addendum, the Islamic law is flexible and rigid in nature. Hence, the concept of “unity in diversity and diversity in unity”. Howbeit, the nagging questions include, inter alia, whether its substantive provisions are subject to changes and interpolations? Whether its principles are static? Hence, the focus of this short but concise article. It seeks to capture how the Islamic law, as an independent system of law, has been able to survive between its flexibility and rigidity nature.

UNITY IN DIVERSITY: WHAT IT MEANS?

The Sharii`ah governs the personal and interpersonal affairs of its adherents. Its doctrines are rigid. It gives no room for modification and amendment of its basic principles as spelled out in the two primary sources of the law. The provisions of the law are so sacred. It originated from the Law Giver, the supreme being.

Taking recourse back to the sources of the Islamic principles, the Qur-aan and the Sunnah remain the two primary sources of the law. The Qur-aan is a divine manual. It contains solely the words of Allaah; no addition and no subtraction. It is revealed to the prophet (SAW) as guidelines to mankind. The second primary source of the Islamic doctrines is the Sunnah of prophet Muhammad (SAW). It further expounds the first primary source, the Qur-aan. There are six major authentic books of prophet traditions: Sohiih Bukahri, Sohiih Muslim, Sunan Abii Dawúd, Sunan An-Nasaai, Sunan ibn Maajah and Sunan Tirmidhi.

By unity in diversity, this encode the unification of the primary sources of the Islamic law, the substantive law itself. The provisions of the Qur-aan in addition to those authentic traditions reported and recorded in the six aforementioned books, sihhatus-sittah, and other authenticated books of ahaadiith are but substantive provisions of the Islamic code. They can not be altered. The same Islamic substantive provisions apply to all and sundry notwithstanding the country one belongs to.

DIVERSITY IN UNITY: WHAT IT MEANS?

Flowing from the above, it is crystal clear that the Islamic substantive provisions as contained in the Qur-aan and the Sunnah remain unaltered. It can not be subjected to amendment nor be reviewed. Notwithstanding the rigidity nature of the substantive provisions, the interpretations and applications of the law is diverse. Hence, the attribute of its diversity nature.

Diversity in unity, stricto senso, connotes the diverse ways in which the Islamic substantive law is interpreted and applied. The law itself remains unchanged right from its existence till now and it shall remain so till eternity. However, juristic approach and analysis may differ. For only in one authority (Quranic verses or prophetic traditions), interpretation and application at different locations and circumstances may differ. This shows the comprehensive nature of the law.

Moreover, in the existing world of Islamic jurisprudence, there are quite number of Islamic jurists who studied the substantive provisions, interpretation and application. However, they differ in some instances. They do differ only in the interpretation and application of the substantive law. This gave birth to the various madhaahib (Islamic jurisprudence) – the classic and the contemporary. The classical schools comprise of the four major madhaahib: Hanafiy, Malikiy, Shaafi`iy and the Hambaliy. The contemporary school includes the Zoohiriy propounded by Daawuud Az-zoohiriy. A clear distinction which exists between the classic and the contemporary schools in terms of how they perceive, understand and interpret the unchangeable unified substantive law of Islamic Sharii`ah.

It is very imperative to state here that, differences of juristic opinions, ikh-tilaaf, is not a new thing; it is in existence even during the lifetime of the prophet (SAW). A very typical example was the ikh-tilaaf among the delegated companions to Banuu-Quroyzoh as to the understanding of the order given by the prophet. The order was that they should not observe `Asr but at Banuu-Quroyzoh. Some of the companions interpreted it contextually and thus, understood it to mean that they were urged to move fast and as quick as they can; and while the other look at it literally and thus, interpreted it to mean that the prophet (SAW) deliberately forbade them to observe `Asr until they reach their destination. Thus, unity in diversity and diversity in unity. While the first party stopped and observe the `Asr prayer, although, they had not reached their destination, the other party waited for them and, after they completed their solaah, they both made progress. More interestingly, they related what transpired to the prophet (SAW) when they got back to Madiinah and, the prophet (SAW) acknowledged and never berate anyone {See Hadiith of al-Bukhori available at accessed on 15th December 2021}. And so many other examples

In essence, the Islamic law, being a universal code, is supreme over all persons, bodies and authorities. However, it accommodates different but monitored and regulated interpretation approaches and its application as the circumstance warrants. Hence, the emergence of the secondary sources of the Islamic law. These secondary sources are the instruments which regulates any diversity as regards the interpretations and application of the substantive Islamic laws. They include ijmaa` (jurist consensus), qiyaas (analogy), saddu adh-dharii`ah (blocking the means to evil), etc which are not covered in this piece. It is equally very pertinent to note that some of these sources are still subject to debate. Like the Zoohiriy, they, virtually, counter most of these secondary sources.

CONCLUSION

Conclusively, the concept shows the rigidity nature of the substantive Islamic law and the flexibility of its interpretation. This makes the law unique as opposed to other systems of law. Since its inception, it has not been subjected to review and nor would it be. Hence, the interplay between the primary and secondary sources of the Islamic Jurisprudence birthed the concept of “unity in diversity and diversity in unity” as a distinctive characteristic of the Islamic Law (Sharii`ah).
